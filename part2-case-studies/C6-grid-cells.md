# C6 — Grid cells → lattices, modular codes, and predictive maps
{: .no_toc }

> **Circuit.** Medial entorhinal cortex layer II/III, organized into a small number of discrete *modules*, each containing cells whose firing tiles space on a common triangular lattice with a common scale and orientation but all possible phases.
> **Primitive extracted.** Two candidates, both defensible. (A) A 2-D continuous attractor on a torus, path-integrating velocity into a lattice phase, with the module ensemble forming a redundant residue-like code of exponential range and intrinsic error correction. (B) The eigenbasis of a *successor operator* — a reinforcement-learning predictive map whose spectral decomposition happens to look like lattices.
> **Status.** The mechanism (A) is close to settled after the recovery of toroidal topology in population activity. The *function* is not: (B) explains a growing pile of reward- and geometry-dependent effects that (A) cannot. This unit is the course's central worked example of one circuit carrying two incompatible level-2 readings, and of how you would tell them apart.

## 1. The phenomenon

A rat forages in a 2 m box. A single neuron in medial entorhinal cortex fires at a set of locations that form a near-perfect triangular lattice covering the entire enclosure (Hafting, Fyhn, Molden, Moser & Moser 2005). Not a place field. Not several place fields. A *lattice*, extending to the walls, with a well-defined spacing, orientation, and phase.

Four facts constrain everything below.

**Triangular, not square.** Hexagonal to high precision. This is not a free parameter of any model in this unit — it falls out of all of them, which will turn out to matter.

**Modular, with discrete scale ratios.** Grid scale increases along the dorsoventral axis, but *piecewise*: it jumps between a handful (typically 4–5 per animal) of discrete modules (Stensola et al. 2012). Successive modules have a scale ratio of about **1.42** — suspiciously close to $\sqrt2$, and suspiciously constant across animals. Cells within a module share scale *and* orientation, differing only in phase.

**Rigid.** Within a module, the relative phase offset between any two cells is preserved across environments, across sleep, and across global remapping of downstream place cells (Fyhn et al. 2007; Yoon et al. 2013). Modules rescale and rotate as units. The single most attractor-like fact in mammalian neuroscience.

**Toroidal.** A single module's population activity, embedded and analysed with persistent homology, has the Betti numbers of a 2-torus — $\beta_0=1,\beta_1=2,\beta_2=1$ — during foraging, REM sleep, and slow-wave sleep (Gardner et al. 2022). The prediction preceded the measurement by thirteen years.

That last point is rare enough to pause on. A model predicted the *topology of a state space*; a mathematical technique was imported and adapted to measure topology from spike trains; the answer came back right. That is what this conversion looks like when it goes well.

## 2. The conversion

### 2.1 The lattice, and why it is hexagonal

A grid cell's rate map is well approximated by a sum of three plane waves:

$$g(\mathbf x) \;\propto\; \sum_{j=1}^{3}\cos\big(\mathbf k_j\cdot(\mathbf x - \mathbf c)\big),\qquad |\mathbf k_j|=k_0,\qquad \mathbf k_1+\mathbf k_2+\mathbf k_3 = 0 .$$

The closure condition $\sum_j\mathbf k_j=0$ forces the three wave-vectors to be 120° apart, and it is the *only* thing that distinguishes hexagons from stripes. Maxima occur where all three phases are simultaneously $0\bmod2\pi$, i.e. on the dual of the lattice generated by $\{\mathbf k_1,\mathbf k_2\}$ — a triangular lattice $\Lambda$ with nearest-neighbour spacing

$$\lambda = \frac{4\pi}{\sqrt3\,k_0}.$$

Why does biology pick the closed triple? Because in *any* isotropic pattern-forming system with a quadratic nonlinearity, the amplitude equations contain a resonant term $\propto A_1^*A_2^*A_3^*$ that exists only when $\mathbf k_1+\mathbf k_2+\mathbf k_3=0$, and that term makes hexagons win over stripes at onset. This is standard Turing/Rayleigh–Bénard bifurcation theory, and it means hexagonality is **not evidence for any particular grid-cell model**. Every model in this unit produces hexagons, and none of them deserves credit for it. Do not let anyone sell you hexagons as a prediction.

The parameters of a module are therefore $(\lambda,\theta,\mathbf c)$ with $\mathbf c \in \mathbb R^2/\Lambda$. That quotient is a **2-torus**, and it is the object the rest of the unit is about.

### 2.2 Reading A: a continuous attractor on a twisted torus

Take the [C4](C4-continuous-attractors.md) machinery and add a dimension. Neurons live on a 2-D sheet at positions $\mathbf r_i$; each is assigned one of four preferred directions $\hat e_i\in\{\hat x,-\hat x,\hat y,-\hat y\}$. Burak & Fiete (2009):

$$\tau\,\dot s_i = -s_i + \phi\Big[\sum_j W_{ij}s_j + B_i(t)\Big],\qquad W_{ij}=W_0\big(\|\mathbf r_i-\mathbf r_j-\ell\,\hat e_i\|\big),\qquad B_i = 1+\alpha\,\hat e_i\!\cdot\!\mathbf v(t),$$

with $W_0$ a centre-surround (Mexican hat) kernel and $\ell$ a small outward shift. Two things happen.

**Pattern formation.** With $\mathbf v=0$ and $\ell=0$ the kernel is translation-invariant on the sheet, and linear stability picks the Fourier ring $|\mathbf k|=k_0$ maximizing $\hat W_0$. The quadratic nonlinearity of $\phi$ then selects the hexagonal triple, exactly as in §2.1. The network spontaneously forms a triangular lattice of activity bumps *on the neural sheet*.

**Velocity coupling.** The direction-dependent shift $\ell\hat e_i$ combined with the direction-dependent gain $\alpha\hat e_i\cdot\mathbf v$ is the two-dimensional version of the fly's P-EN offset loop. Repeating the [C4 §2.4](C4-continuous-attractors.md) computation componentwise: averaging over the four sub-populations, the effective kernel acquires a term $-\alpha\ell\,(\mathbf v\cdot\nabla)W_0$, and the same Fredholm solvability argument on the $2$-dimensional zero-mode subspace $\{\partial_x s^*,\partial_y s^*\}$ gives

$$\boxed{\;\dot{\mathbf c} = \frac{\alpha\ell}{\tau}\,\mathbf v\;}\qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad \mathbf c(t)=\mathbf c(0)+\frac{\alpha\ell}{\tau}\int_0^t\mathbf v\,dt' \pmod\Lambda .$$

Two-dimensional path integration, with the identical structure as the fly heading system: a continuous symmetry ($\mathbb R^2$ translation on the sheet, quotiented by the pattern's own periodicity $\Lambda$) supplies a manifold of fixed points, and a velocity-gated antisymmetric term slides the state along it.

**Why "twisted".** The state space of the module is $\mathbb R^2/\Lambda$. To make a *finite* network realize this exactly, you give the neural sheet periodic boundary conditions matched to $\Lambda$ — but the fundamental domain of a triangular lattice is a rhombus, and gluing a rhombus's opposite edges gives a torus whose identification involves a shear. Hence "twisted torus" (Guanella, Kiper & Verschure 2007). Burak & Fiete showed the twist is not needed if the sheet is large enough with aperiodic boundaries, because the bulk pattern is what matters; but the *state space* is a torus either way, which is what Gardner et al. measured.

The [C4](C4-continuous-attractors.md) fragility analysis carries over verbatim and is worse here: two marginal directions, so heterogeneity generically leaves a small number of isolated fixed points on $\mathbb T^2$ (Poincaré–Hopf now gives $\sum\text{index}=\chi(\mathbb T^2)=0$, so saddles and nodes balance), and noise diffuses the phase in two dimensions. Path integration error grows as $\sqrt t$ at best, which is why the system must be periodically re-anchored by boundary and landmark input.

### 2.3 Reading A′: the grid code as a residue number system

Now use several modules. Module $m$ with scale $\lambda_m$ reports only the phase

$$\mathbf c_m = \mathbf x \bmod \Lambda_m .$$

This is *precisely* a residue representation. Suppose for a moment the $\lambda_m$ were pairwise coprime integers in 1-D. The Chinese Remainder Theorem says $\mathbf x$ is uniquely determined on $[0,\prod_m\lambda_m)$: range **multiplicative** in the moduli, hence **exponential in the number of modules**, while the neuron count is only *additive*. Compare:

| Code | Neurons for range $L$ at resolution $\delta$ |
|---|---|
| Place cells (one unit per location) | $L/\delta$ |
| Grid modules ($M$ modules, $n$ cells each) | $\sim M\,n$ with $L/\delta \sim n^{M}$, i.e. $\;M\log n$ neurons per bit |

Fiete, Burak & Brookings (2008) made this argument quantitatively; Sreenivasan & Fiete (2011) turned it into a coding-theoretic statement: with more modules than strictly needed, the code is a *redundant* residue number system, and redundant RNS codes have genuine error-correcting power — a single grossly corrupted residue can be detected and corrected because the true codeword lies on a one-dimensional curve winding in $\mathbb T^{2M}$ and the curve stays far from itself.

**The number theory is not decoration.** Real grid scales are incommensurate reals, so CRT does not literally apply. The right statement comes from simultaneous Diophantine approximation. Encode $x\in\mathbb R$ as $\big(x/\lambda_1,\dots,x/\lambda_M\big)\bmod 1$. An *alias* is a displacement $q$ with $\|q/\lambda_m\|<\sigma$ for all $m$ ($\|\cdot\|$ = distance to nearest integer, $\sigma$ = phase noise). Dirichlet's simultaneous approximation theorem guarantees that for any $N$ there exists $q\le N^M$ with $\|q/\lambda_m\|<1/N$ for every $m$. Setting $\sigma=1/N$:

$$\boxed{\;L_{\text{unambiguous}} \;\lesssim\; \sigma^{-M}\;}$$

Range grows as the $M$-th power of the per-module signal-to-noise — exponential in the number of modules, with the exponent supplied by a theorem from 1842. And the bound is tight for *badly approximable* tuples $(1/\lambda_1,\dots,1/\lambda_M)$, so evolution has an incentive to choose scale ratios that are hard to approximate by rationals. Whether $1.42\approx\sqrt2$ (a quadratic irrational, and badly approximable) is a coincidence, I genuinely do not know; I lean toward coincidence, but it is the kind of coincidence worth checking.

**Optimal ratio.** The clean 1-D argument (Exercise 6.3) gives a preferred scale ratio equal to $e\approx2.72$ under a winner-take-all decoding criterion (Mathis, Herz & Stemmler 2012). Wei, Prentice & Balasubramanian (2015) redo the optimization in 2-D with a proper noise model and obtain $\approx1.4$–1.5, matching the measured 1.42. The *robust* prediction across all these treatments is **geometric progression of scales** — the ratio itself is criterion-dependent and I would not put weight on any particular value. That the observed ratio is constant across animals is a much stronger constraint than that it equals any specific number.

### 2.4 Reading B: grid cells as eigenvectors of the successor representation

Now throw all of that away and start from reinforcement learning.

The *successor representation* (Dayan 1993) of a Markov policy with transition matrix $T$ and discount $\gamma$ is

$$M(s,s') = \mathbb E\Big[\sum_{t=0}^\infty \gamma^t\,\mathbb 1[s_t=s']\;\Big|\;s_0=s\Big] \;=\; \big[(I-\gamma T)^{-1}\big]_{ss'} .$$

$M(s,\cdot)$ is the discounted expected future occupancy from $s$. It is a *predictive* representation: it caches where you are going, not where you are. Its point is that value becomes linear in it, $V = MR$, so a change of reward requires no re-planning — you re-multiply. And $M$ is learnable by TD:

$$\Delta M(s_t,\cdot) = \eta\Big[\mathbb 1[s_t=\cdot] + \gamma M(s_{t+1},\cdot) - M(s_t,\cdot)\Big].$$

Stachenfeld, Botvinick & Gershman (2017) proposed: **hippocampal place fields are rows of $M$, and entorhinal grid cells are its eigenvectors.** The place-field claim explains several things the classical "place field = location" story does not — fields skew backward along a stereotyped running direction (because they encode *future* occupancy), fields bend and clip around barriers, fields distort toward rewarded locations, and field size scales with $\gamma$.

The grid claim is spectral. Since $M=(I-\gamma T)^{-1}$, $M$ and $T$ share eigenvectors, with

$$\mu_i = \frac{1}{1-\gamma\lambda_i}.$$

On a translation-invariant domain — a periodic 2-D environment with a spatially homogeneous random walk — $T$ is a convolution and its eigenvectors are plane waves $e^{i\mathbf k\cdot\mathbf x}$. Periodic bases. Low-frequency ones dominate the spectrum of $M$. In simulation, the leading eigenvectors of an SR learned in an open box look strikingly like grid fields, and — this is the attractive part — in a box with a barrier they *deform around the barrier*, which no path-integration model predicts.

**Where the argument is weaker than advertised.** Plane waves are stripes, not hexagons. To get hexagons you need to combine three degenerate modes with the right relative phases, and eigen-decomposition alone gives you no reason to prefer that combination over any other element of the degenerate subspace. Worse, on a square-lattice world the degeneracy group is $D_4$ (order 8), not $O(2)$, so the natural combinations are *square*, not hexagonal. What actually rescues the story is an extra constraint: Dordek, Soudry, Meir & Derdikman (2016) showed that **non-negative** PCA of place-cell inputs — not ordinary PCA — yields hexagonal grids, and Sorscher, Mel, Ganguli & Ocko (2019) showed that this is again the Turing mechanism of §2.1 in disguise: a nonnegativity constraint plus an isotropic similarity kernel is a pattern-forming system, and pattern-forming systems make hexagons.

So the honest version of Reading B is: *grid cells are the leading modes of an isotropic predictive similarity kernel, subject to nonnegativity.* That is a real and interesting claim. "Eigenvectors of the SR" is a slogan that omits the constraint doing all the work.

### 2.5 Two readings of one circuit — and how to referee them

Here is the methodological heart of the unit, and the reason C6 sits where it does in this course.

| | **A: path-integrating metric** | **B: predictive map** |
|---|---|---|
| Level-1 problem | Estimate self-location from velocity | Estimate discounted future occupancy |
| Representation | Phase on $\mathbb T^2$ per module | Eigenmodes of $(I-\gamma T)^{-1}$ |
| Where structure comes from | Recurrent connectivity (innate/developmental) | Behavioural statistics (learned) |
| Predicts hexagons? | Yes (Turing) | Yes (Turing, once nonnegativity is imposed) |
| Predicts modules? | Only via an efficiency argument bolted on | Yes — bands of the spectrum |
| Predicts rigidity across environments | **Yes, strongly** | No |
| Predicts reward/goal deformation | **No** | Yes |
| Predicts structure during sleep | **Yes** | No reason to |

The two readings are not merely different emphases. They make opposite predictions about whether the grid pattern is a property of the *network* or of the *environment*, and that is testable.

**What the evidence says.**

*For A.* Cell-pair phase offsets are preserved across environments and across remapping (Yoon et al. 2013) — the population lives on a fixed low-dimensional manifold irrespective of what the world looks like. Toroidal topology survives into slow-wave and REM sleep with no sensory input and no behaviour at all (Gardner et al. 2022). Vestibular lesion abolishes grid periodicity (Winter, Clark & Taube 2015) — path integration input is *necessary*. Modules rescale coherently after environment resizing (Barry, Hayman, Burgess & Jeffery 2007; Stensola et al. 2012). This is a strong case, and the sleep result is close to decisive: a predictive map of a policy that is not currently being executed has no business existing on a torus at 3 a.m.

*For B.* Grid maps deform toward rewarded goal locations (Boccara, Nardin, Stella, O'Neill & Csicsvari 2019; Butler, Hardcastle & Giocomo 2019) — the pattern is *not* a rigid metric. Grids shear and lose hexagonal symmetry in polarized enclosures such as trapezoids (Krupic, Bauza, Burton, Barry & O'Keefe 2015). Grid cells appear developmentally *later* than place and head-direction cells (Langston et al. 2010; Wills, Cacucci, Burgess & O'Keefe 2010), consistent with something learned from experience. And place fields do show the specific predictive skew and barrier-bending that the SR predicts and pure metric coding does not.

**My position.** A is the mechanism; B is (part of) the objective. The circuit is a continuous attractor — the topology measurements settle that — but the *reason* to build a toroidal attractor with these particular scales is an efficient-coding argument, and the SR provides a plausible learning objective from which a hexagonal, modular code emerges as the optimum. Sorscher et al.'s pattern-formation account is the reconciliation: both stories bottom out in the same instability, approached from opposite directions. What B genuinely adds that A cannot supply is *plasticity* — the goal and geometry effects — and the correct synthesis is a CAN whose input weights (from boundary and landmark cells) are learned to minimize predictive error. Nobody has written that model down properly.

**The experiment I would run.** Dissociate *geometry* from *transition statistics*. Build an enclosure in which a barrier is visually transparent but impassable, and a second condition in which a visually identical surface is passable. Reading A says grid distortion tracks the visible boundary (via boundary-vector-cell anchoring), and should be identical in both. Reading B says distortion tracks the *transition structure* — large in the impassable case, absent in the passable one. Then repeat the measurement in sleep: A predicts the toroidal manifold is unchanged; B predicts the learned deformation persists in replay. This is straightforward and, as far as I know, nobody has done exactly it.

## 3. Worked example / model to build

Two short programs, one per reading.

```python
import numpy as np
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)

# ================= A. modular grid code: range and error correction =================
lam  = np.array([30., 42., 59., 83., 117.])       # cm; successive ratios ~1.41
L, dx, sig = 3000., 0.5, 0.02                     # range, decode grid, phase noise (fraction)
pos  = np.arange(0., L, dx)
CODE = (pos[:, None] / lam) % 1.0                 # (npos, M) codebook

def cost(ph):                                     # squared circular residual, per module
    d = np.abs(CODE - ph); d = np.minimum(d, 1 - d); return d**2

def dec_plain(ph):  return pos[np.argmin(cost(ph).sum(1))]
def dec_robust(ph):                               # RRNS-style: discard worst-fitting residue
    return pos[np.argmin(np.sort(cost(ph), axis=1)[:, :-1].sum(1))]

x   = rng.uniform(0, L, 300)
ph  = ((x[:, None] / lam) + sig*rng.standard_normal((300, len(lam)))) % 1.0
bad = ph.copy(); bad[:, 2] = rng.random(300)      # destroy module 3 completely

for name, f in [("plain", dec_plain), ("robust", dec_robust)]:
    e, e2 = np.array([f(p) for p in ph]), np.array([f(p) for p in bad])
    print(f"{name:7s} med|err|={np.median(np.abs(e-x)):.2f} cm   "
          f"catastrophic={np.mean(np.abs(e-x)>5):.3f}   "
          f"recovery with 1 module destroyed={np.mean(np.abs(e2-x)<5):.3f}")

# ================= B. successor representation on a 2-D torus =================
n, gam = 24, 0.98
idx = lambda i, j: (i % n)*n + (j % n)
T = np.zeros((n*n, n*n))
for i in range(n):
    for j in range(n):
        for di, dj in [(1,0), (-1,0), (0,1), (0,-1)]:
            T[idx(i,j), idx(i+di, j+dj)] = 0.25
M = np.linalg.inv(np.eye(n*n) - gam*T)
w, V = np.linalg.eigh((M + M.T)/2)
order = np.argsort(-w)
print("top SR eigenvalues:", np.round(w[order[:6]], 2))
E = V[:, order[1]].reshape(n, n)                  # <- plot this: a periodic BAND, not a hexagon
print("eigenvector 2 row-0:", np.round(E[0], 2))
```

**What to look for.**

1. **Exponential range.** With 5 modules and 2% phase noise the decoder nails 30 m to a median error of ~0.3 cm with *zero* catastrophic failures, even though the largest module has a period of 1.17 m. Now delete modules one at a time (`lam = lam[:4]`, `lam[:3]`, …) and re-run: catastrophic rate goes $0.000 \to 0.000 \to 0.15 \to 0.96$ for $M=5,4,3,2$. Range is multiplicative — each module you remove costs a *factor*, not a subtraction.
2. **Error correction requires outlier rejection, not just redundancy.** Destroy one module's phase entirely. The plain least-squares decoder recovers only ~39% of positions; the robust decoder, which simply discards the single worst-fitting residue, recovers ~92%. This is the whole point of a *redundant* residue number system, and it is easy to miss: extra modules do not automatically buy robustness, because a squared-error decoder lets one grossly wrong residue veto the correct answer. You must build a decoder that can *reject* a residue. Whether any brain does so — and where — is Open Problem 2 below. Corrupt *two* modules and find where the robust decoder breaks: that is your empirical minimum distance.
3. **Scale-ratio scan.** Replace `lam` with a geometric series $\lambda_1r^{m}$ and scan $r\in[1.1,3]$ at fixed $M$ and fixed per-module resolution. The achievable range has a *broad* optimum in $\log r$ — which is §2.3's point: agreement with any particular ratio is weak evidence.
4. **SR eigenvectors are stripes.** Reshape and plot `V[:, order[k]]` for $k=1,\dots,8$: periodic bands with square-lattice symmetry, *not* hexagons. The degeneracy is visible in the printed spectrum (the code returns $50.0$, then $27.25$ four times). Now impose nonnegativity — project onto $\max(\cdot,0)$ and re-orthogonalize, or run NMF on $M$ — and hexagonal structure appears. The constraint, not the spectrum, makes grids.
5. Insert a barrier by zeroing transitions across a line, recompute $M$, compare eigenvectors. They deform. Ask what Reading A predicts for the same manipulation.

## 4. Exercises

**★ E6.1 — Three waves make a triangular lattice.** Show that $g(\mathbf x)=\sum_{j=1}^3\cos(\mathbf k_j\cdot\mathbf x)$ with $|\mathbf k_j|=k_0$ and $\sum_j\mathbf k_j=0$ has maxima on a triangular lattice of spacing $\lambda=4\pi/(\sqrt3k_0)$, and that dropping the closure condition gives stripes or a square lattice instead.

<details markdown="1"><summary>Solution</summary>

**Geometry of the $\mathbf k$'s.** Three vectors of equal length summing to zero must form an equilateral triangle, so they are at mutual angles of 120°: $\mathbf k_1=k_0(1,0)$, $\mathbf k_2=k_0(-\tfrac12,\tfrac{\sqrt3}{2})$, $\mathbf k_3=-\mathbf k_1-\mathbf k_2=k_0(-\tfrac12,-\tfrac{\sqrt3}{2})$.

**Maxima.** $g\le3$ with equality iff $\mathbf k_j\cdot\mathbf x\in2\pi\mathbb Z$ for all $j$. Because $\mathbf k_3=-\mathbf k_1-\mathbf k_2$, only two conditions are independent. The solution set is $\Lambda=\{\mathbf x: \mathbf k_1\!\cdot\!\mathbf x, \mathbf k_2\!\cdot\!\mathbf x\in2\pi\mathbb Z\}$ — the dual lattice of the one generated by $\mathbf k_1,\mathbf k_2$. Solving $\mathbf a_i\cdot\mathbf k_j=2\pi\delta_{ij}$:
$$\mathbf a_1=\frac{2\pi}{k_0}\Big(1,\tfrac{1}{\sqrt3}\Big),\qquad \mathbf a_2=\frac{2\pi}{k_0}\Big(0,\tfrac{2}{\sqrt3}\Big),$$
with $|\mathbf a_1|=|\mathbf a_2|=|\mathbf a_1-\mathbf a_2|=\frac{4\pi}{\sqrt3 k_0}$ — equilateral. Hence triangular with spacing $\lambda=4\pi/(\sqrt3k_0)$. Sanity: $\lambda k_0 = 4\pi/\sqrt3\approx7.26$, so the grid spacing is a little more than one wavelength ($2\pi/k_0$) — correct for a hexagonal lattice.

**Without closure.** Two waves at 90° give $\cos(k_0x)+\cos(k_0y)$: a square lattice. Two waves at any angle give a rhombic lattice. One wave gives stripes. Three waves whose sum is nonzero give a quasi-pattern with no exact common maxima; the function is not lattice-periodic at all unless the $\mathbf k$'s lie in a rank-2 lattice.

**Why nature closes the triple.** In an isotropic pattern-forming system the amplitude equations for modes on the critical ring contain a quadratic resonance $\dot A_1 \supset \Gamma A_2^*A_3^*$ that is nonzero only when $\mathbf k_1+\mathbf k_2+\mathbf k_3=0$. That term lowers the energy of the hexagonal state below the striped one at onset, so hexagons are selected generically. Consequence for this unit: hexagonality discriminates nothing between competing grid-cell theories.
</details>

**★★ E6.2 — Exponential range from Dirichlet.** State and use Dirichlet's simultaneous approximation theorem to show that a modular code with $M$ modules and per-module phase resolution $\sigma$ has unambiguous range $\lesssim\sigma^{-M}$, and give the neuron count as a function of range.

<details markdown="1"><summary>Solution</summary>

**Theorem (Dirichlet, simultaneous version).** For reals $\alpha_1,\dots,\alpha_M$ and any integer $N\ge2$, there exists an integer $q$ with $1\le q\le N^M$ such that $\|q\alpha_m\| < 1/N$ for all $m$, where $\|t\|$ denotes the distance from $t$ to the nearest integer.

*Proof sketch (pigeonhole).* Consider the $N^M+1$ points $\big(\{q\alpha_1\},\dots,\{q\alpha_M\}\big)$ for $q=0,\dots,N^M$ in the unit cube $[0,1)^M$, partitioned into $N^M$ sub-cubes of side $1/N$. Two points share a cube; their difference gives the required $q$.

**Application.** Encode $x$ (an integer number of resolution units) by phases $\phi_m=\{x\alpha_m\}$ with $\alpha_m=1/\lambda_m$. An alias is a displacement $q\ne0$ that is indistinguishable, i.e. $\|q\alpha_m\|<\sigma$ for all $m$. Setting $N=\lceil1/\sigma\rceil$, Dirichlet guarantees such a $q$ with $q\le\sigma^{-M}$. Hence

$$L_{\text{unambiguous}}\;\le\;\sigma^{-M}.$$

The bound is achieved (up to constants) when $(\alpha_1,\dots,\alpha_M)$ is *badly approximable*, i.e. $\liminf_q q^{1/M}\max_m\|q\alpha_m\|>0$; almost all tuples are not badly approximable in the strong sense, but the failure is only by logarithmic factors for typical tuples.

**Neuron count.** Give module $m$ enough cells to resolve its phase to $\sigma$: in 1-D, $n\approx 1/\sigma$ cells (in 2-D, $n\approx1/\sigma^2$). Total $N_{\rm cells}=Mn = M/\sigma$. Then

$$L \le \sigma^{-M} = \Big(\frac{M}{N_{\rm cells}}\Big)^{-M} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \log L \le M\log\frac{N_{\rm cells}}{M}.$$

Compare a place-cell code, which needs $N_{\rm cells}=L$ (in resolution units) so $\log L = \log N_{\rm cells}$. The modular code buys a factor of $M$ in the exponent. Concretely: to cover $L=10^6$ resolution units, place cells need $10^6$ neurons; five modules with $\sigma=1/16$ need $5\times16=80$ and cover $16^5\approx10^6$. Four orders of magnitude. **This, not hexagonality, is the argument for grid codes.**
</details>

**★★ E6.3 — Optimal scale ratio.** In 1-D, a nested code has module scales $\lambda_m=\lambda_1r^{m-1}$, $m=1..M$, with $n$ cells per module resolving each phase into $n$ bins. Unambiguous decoding requires that module $m+1$'s resolution be finer than module $m$'s period. Maximize range/resolution at fixed total cell count and find $r$.

<details markdown="1"><summary>Solution</summary>

**Constraint.** Module $m+1$ localizes $x$ to within $\lambda_{m+1}/n$. To identify *which* period of module $m$ you are in, you need $\lambda_{m+1}/n < \lambda_m$, i.e. $r<n$.

**Objective.** Final resolution is set by the smallest module: $\delta=\lambda_1/n$. Range is set by the largest: $L=\lambda_M=\lambda_1r^{M-1}$. So

$$\frac{L}{\delta} = n\,r^{M-1}.$$

**Optimization.** Total cells $N=Mn$. Saturate the constraint, $r=n$ (any smaller $r$ wastes resolution). Then $L/\delta = n^{M}$ and

$$\log\frac{L}{\delta}=M\log n = \frac{N}{n}\log n .$$

Maximize $\log n / n$: $\frac{d}{dn}\big(n^{-1}\log n\big)= n^{-2}(1-\log n)=0 \Rightarrow \boxed{n=e},\;\; r=e\approx2.72,$
and then $\log(L/\delta) = N/e$, i.e. **range exponential in the number of neurons**, $L/\delta = e^{N/e}$.

**Reality check.** The measured ratio is $\approx1.42$, not $2.72$. Three reasons the idealization is off, all instructive:
1. *Noise.* Saturating $r=n$ leaves zero margin; any phase noise causes catastrophic decoding failures. Requiring the resolution to be a factor $\kappa$ finer than needed replaces $r=n$ with $r=n/\kappa$, pushing the optimum down.
2. *Dimension.* In 2-D a module with $n$ cells resolves phase to $\sim n^{-1/2}$ per dimension, so the exponent changes; Wei, Prentice & Balasubramanian (2015) redo this properly and get $\approx1.4$–1.5.
3. *Decoder.* $n=e$ assumes ideal winner-take-all decoding. A biologically plausible decoder (linear readout, or a downstream attractor) is less efficient and prefers more redundancy, i.e. smaller $r$.

The lesson is not that the theory fails but that the *optimum is broad in $\log r$* — $\log(L/\delta)$ at $r=1.4$ is within a factor of ~2 of its value at $r=e$ for realistic $M$ — so agreement with 1.42 is weak evidence. What is strong evidence is the *existence of a geometric progression* with a ratio that is constant within an animal.
</details>

**★★ E6.4 — SR spectrum.** Prove that $M=(I-\gamma T)^{-1}$ and $T$ have the same eigenvectors with $\mu=1/(1-\gamma\lambda)$. On a ring of $n$ states with a symmetric nearest-neighbour walk, compute the full spectrum and identify which eigenvectors dominate $M$ as $\gamma\to1$.

<details markdown="1"><summary>Solution</summary>

**Shared eigenvectors.** If $T\mathbf u=\lambda\mathbf u$ then $(I-\gamma T)\mathbf u=(1-\gamma\lambda)\mathbf u$, and provided $1-\gamma\lambda\ne0$, $(I-\gamma T)^{-1}\mathbf u = (1-\gamma\lambda)^{-1}\mathbf u$. Since $\|T\|_\infty=1$ and $\gamma<1$, no eigenvalue of $I-\gamma T$ vanishes, so the map $\lambda\mapsto\mu=1/(1-\gamma\lambda)$ is a bijection on the spectrum and eigenvectors are unchanged. Equivalently, $M=\sum_{t\ge0}\gamma^tT^t$ is a power series in $T$.

**Ring.** $T$ is circulant with symbol $\tfrac12(e^{i\theta}+e^{-i\theta})$, so eigenvectors are $u^{(k)}_j=e^{2\pi ikj/n}$ with

$$\lambda_k=\cos\frac{2\pi k}{n},\qquad \mu_k=\frac{1}{1-\gamma\cos(2\pi k/n)},\qquad k=0,\dots,n-1 .$$

Real eigenvectors: $\cos(2\pi kj/n)$ and $\sin(2\pi kj/n)$, degenerate in pairs for $0<k<n/2$.

**As $\gamma\to1$.** $\mu_0=1/(1-\gamma)\to\infty$ (the uniform mode; discard it, it carries no spatial information). The next largest is $k=1$: $\mu_1=[1-\gamma\cos(2\pi/n)]^{-1}$. For small $2\pi k/n$, $\mu_k\approx[1-\gamma+\gamma(2\pi k/n)^2/2]^{-1}$ — i.e. a Lorentzian in $k$ with width $\propto\sqrt{(1-\gamma)}$. So the SR spectrum is dominated by *low spatial frequencies*, with a cutoff wavenumber

$$k_c \sim \frac{n}{2\pi}\sqrt{\frac{2(1-\gamma)}{\gamma}} .$$

Two consequences. (i) The dominant eigenvectors are long-wavelength periodic functions — the "grid-like" claim, in its honest 1-D form: they are *sinusoids*, and their spatial period is set by $\gamma$ and the environment size. (ii) The eigenvalue spectrum has a *continuum*, not discrete bands, so the modular organization of real grid cells does not fall out of the SR without additional assumptions. Getting discrete modules from a continuous spectrum requires something else — e.g. sparse/nonnegative dictionary learning, which quantizes.
</details>

**★★★ E6.5 — Why eigen-decomposition alone cannot give hexagons.** Compute the spectrum of the 4-neighbour random walk on an $n\times n$ torus. Show that generic eigenspaces are $D_4$-symmetric, that any hexagonal function is *not* in a single eigenspace, and identify precisely what extra structure is needed.

<details markdown="1"><summary>Solution</summary>

**Spectrum.** $T$ is a 2-D circulant; eigenvectors $u^{(\mathbf k)}_{ij}=e^{2\pi i(k_1i+k_2j)/n}$ with

$$\lambda_{\mathbf k}=\tfrac12\Big[\cos\tfrac{2\pi k_1}{n}+\cos\tfrac{2\pi k_2}{n}\Big].$$

**Degeneracy.** $\lambda_{\mathbf k}$ is invariant under $k_1\leftrightarrow k_2$ and $k_i\to-k_i$: the group $D_4$ of order 8. For generic $\mathbf k$ with $k_1\ne k_2$, $k_i\ne0$, the orbit has 8 elements, giving an 8-dimensional (real) eigenspace spanned by $\{\cos,\sin\}$ of $(k_1,k_2)$, $(k_2,k_1)$, $(k_1,-k_2)$, $(k_2,-k_1)$. But there are *accidental* degeneracies too whenever $\cos a+\cos b=\cos a'+\cos b'$, which for generic $n$ produce larger, non-symmetry-related eigenspaces.

**Hexagons are not eigenvectors.** A hexagonal pattern needs three wave-vectors of equal modulus $|\mathbf k_j|=k_0$ at 120°. On the square lattice, $\lambda_{\mathbf k}$ depends on $\mathbf k$ through $\cos k_1+\cos k_2$, which is *not* a function of $|\mathbf k|$: it is only $D_4$-invariant. Three vectors at 120° with equal modulus generically have **different** eigenvalues, so their sum is not an eigenvector at all. The hexagonal function lies in a sum of distinct eigenspaces and will be destroyed by the dynamics.

**What is needed.**
1. *Isotropy.* Replace the square-lattice walk by one whose transition kernel depends only on distance (a continuum diffusion, or a place-cell similarity kernel $\exp(-\|\mathbf x-\mathbf x'\|^2/2s^2)$). Then $\lambda$ depends only on $|\mathbf k|$, and *every* $\mathbf k$ on a critical ring is degenerate — an $O(2)$-degenerate eigenspace, infinite-dimensional in the continuum. Now hexagons live inside a single eigenspace.
2. *A selection principle.* $O(2)$ degeneracy means the eigen-decomposition is completely indifferent between stripes, squares, hexagons, and any other superposition on the ring. Something must break the tie. Nonnegativity does it: Dordek et al. (2016) show that non-negative PCA of place-cell inputs (equivalently, PCA under $u\ge0$) selects the hexagonal combination, because among unit-norm functions on the critical ring the hexagonal triple maximizes the ratio of peak height to mean under a nonnegativity constraint — exactly the quadratic-resonance argument of E6.1 in a different guise.

**Verdict.** "Grid cells are eigenvectors of the successor representation" is, taken literally, false: on any non-isotropic transition structure they are not, and on an isotropic one the eigen-decomposition does not single them out. The true statement is "grid cells are the leading modes of an isotropic predictive kernel under a nonnegativity constraint," and the nonnegativity is not a technicality — it is the entire hexagon-producing mechanism, and it is the same mechanism as the continuous-attractor model's. This is worth internalizing as a general habit: when a spectral story produces a very specific pattern, ask which constraint actually broke the degeneracy.
</details>

**★★ E6.6 — 2-D velocity coupling.** Derive $\dot{\mathbf c}=(\alpha\ell/\tau)\mathbf v$ for the Burak–Fiete network, mirroring the [C4](C4-continuous-attractors.md) §2.4 computation. Why does the 4-population structure (rather than 2) matter?

<details markdown="1"><summary>Solution</summary>

Write the continuum version on the neural sheet, with four sub-populations indexed by $\hat e\in\{\pm\hat x,\pm\hat y\}$ and drive $B_{\hat e}=1+\alpha\hat e\cdot\mathbf v$:

$$\tau\partial_ts_{\hat e}(\mathbf r) = -s_{\hat e}+\phi\Big[\sum_{\hat e'}\int d\mathbf r'\,W_0(\|\mathbf r-\mathbf r'-\ell\hat e\|)\,s_{\hat e'}(\mathbf r') + 1+\alpha\hat e\!\cdot\!\mathbf v\Big].$$

Assume the four sub-populations carry the same pattern up to the small shifts (true to $O(\ell,\alpha v)$), and let $s(\mathbf r)=\sum_{\hat e}s_{\hat e}(\mathbf r)$. Expanding the shifted kernel, $W_0(\|\mathbf r-\mathbf r'-\ell\hat e\|)= W_0 - \ell\,\hat e\cdot\nabla W_0 + O(\ell^2)$, and weighting each population by its drive $\propto(1+\alpha\hat e\cdot\mathbf v)$:

$$W_{\rm eff} = \sum_{\hat e}\tfrac14(1+\alpha\hat e\!\cdot\!\mathbf v)\big(W_0-\ell\,\hat e\!\cdot\!\nabla W_0\big) = W_0 - \tfrac{\alpha\ell}{4}\sum_{\hat e}(\hat e\!\cdot\!\mathbf v)(\hat e\!\cdot\!\nabla)W_0 + O(\ell^2,\alpha^2).$$

The key sum: for the four unit vectors $\pm\hat x,\pm\hat y$, $\sum_{\hat e}\hat e_a\hat e_b = 2\delta_{ab}$. Hence $\sum_{\hat e}(\hat e\cdot\mathbf v)(\hat e\cdot\nabla)=2\,\mathbf v\cdot\nabla$, giving

$$W_{\rm eff}=W_0-\tfrac{\alpha\ell}{2}\,(\mathbf v\cdot\nabla)W_0 .$$

Now the same solvability argument as C4. The fixed-point pattern $s^*$ has a **two**-dimensional zero-mode space $\{\partial_xs^*,\partial_ys^*\}$ (two continuous translation symmetries). The perturbation acts as $\delta I = -\tfrac{\alpha\ell}{2}(\mathbf v\cdot\nabla)(W_0*\phi(s^*)) = -\tfrac{\alpha\ell}{2}(\mathbf v\cdot\nabla)s^*$ up to the constant drive. Projecting onto the left null vectors $v_a=\phi'(s^*)\partial_as^*$ and using $\langle v_a,\partial_bs^*\rangle = \mathcal N\delta_{ab}$ (which holds because the hexagonal pattern's stress tensor is isotropic — see below), the shape factors cancel exactly as in 1-D:

$$\dot c_a = -\frac{\langle v_a,\delta I\rangle}{\tau\,\mathcal N} = \frac{\alpha\ell}{2\tau}\,v_a .$$

(The factor $\tfrac12$ versus $1$ depends on normalization conventions for the drive; the content is $\dot{\mathbf c}\propto\mathbf v$ with a gain $\propto\alpha\ell/\tau$.)

**Why four populations.** Two things.
1. *Isotropy of the gain.* With only two sub-populations ($\pm\hat x$), $\sum\hat e_a\hat e_b=2\delta_{ax}\delta_{bx}$, so only the $x$-component of velocity drives motion: you get a 1-D integrator embedded in 2-D. Four populations give $\sum_{\hat e}\hat e_a\hat e_b\propto\delta_{ab}$, an isotropic tensor, so the gain is direction-independent. Any set of $\ge3$ directions equally spaced on the circle works — the requirement is that $\sum_{\hat e}\hat e\otimes\hat e\propto I$, i.e. the directions form a *tight frame*. (Three directions at 120° suffice, and there is a nice speculation there about why conjunctive grid×head-direction cells might come in three or six preferred directions rather than four.)
2. *Cancellation at rest.* At $\mathbf v=0$ the four shifted kernels average back to an unshifted symmetric one, so the pattern is stationary. With an odd or unbalanced set you get spontaneous drift — which is exactly [C4](C4-continuous-attractors.md)'s $h_0\ne0$ failure mode, now in 2-D.

**Isotropy of $\langle v_a,\partial_bs^*\rangle$.** For a hexagonal pattern, this tensor is invariant under the pattern's point group $C_6$, and any $C_6$-invariant symmetric 2-tensor is proportional to $\delta_{ab}$ (since $C_6\supset C_3$ and the only $C_3$-invariant symmetric 2-tensors in 2-D are multiples of the identity). So hexagonal symmetry *guarantees* isotropic path-integration gain. A square lattice would too; a striped pattern would not, and a striped-pattern integrator would path-integrate only along one axis. Add this to the list of reasons hexagons are the right answer.
</details>

**★★ E6.7 — Computational: design the discriminating experiment.** Using the Section 3 SR code, insert a barrier and quantify how much the leading eigenvectors deform. Then state what Reading A predicts for the identical manipulation, and compute how large the effect must be to be detectable given typical grid-score noise.

<details markdown="1"><summary>Solution</summary>

**Simulation.** Zero the transitions crossing the line $j=n/2$ for $i<n/2$ (a half-wall), renormalize each row of $T$ to sum to 1, recompute $M=(I-\gamma T)^{-1}$, re-eigendecompose. Measure deformation as $1-|\langle u_k^{\rm wall},u_k^{\rm open}\rangle|$ after matching eigenvectors by maximum overlap.

Expected result: leading eigenvectors acquire a visible discontinuity in wavefront orientation across the wall, and the deformation grows with $\gamma$ (a longer horizon means the wall affects occupancy over a larger region). For $\gamma=0.98$, $n=24$, overlap losses of 0.2–0.5 for the first ten non-uniform modes are typical. Grid-like periodicity is *locally* preserved but the phase relationship across the wall is broken — the two sides become nearly independent maps.

**Reading A's prediction.** In a continuous-attractor path integrator, the phase is carried by network dynamics and is continuous along any path the animal walks. Walking around the end of the half-wall carries the phase continuously, so the two sides must be *phase-consistent* up to accumulated path-integration error. A prediction with teeth: the phase offset measured across the wall should equal the offset predicted by integrating along the shortest *walkable* path, and should be independent of whether the wall is opaque or transparent, passable or not, rewarded or not.

**Reading B's prediction.** The offset is whatever the transition statistics say, so it depends on passability and on the animal's policy — and it should *change* if the animal's route habits change while the geometry is fixed.

**Effect size.** Grid phase is measurable to roughly $\sigma_\phi\approx0.05$–0.1 of a period from a well-isolated cell with ~20 min of foraging (limited by field jitter, not spike count). Simultaneously recorded modules of $n$ cells give phase estimates $\sim\sigma_\phi/\sqrt n$; with $n=30$ co-modular cells, $\approx0.01$–0.02 periods. Reading B's predicted deformation, from the simulation, corresponds to phase shifts of order $0.1$–$0.3$ of a period across the barrier. So the effect is roughly **10–20 standard errors** — comfortably detectable. This is not a hard experiment. That it has not been done in exactly this form is the interesting fact.

**The extra condition that makes it decisive.** Run the same recording during subsequent sleep and apply the [S-05](../structures/S-05-toroidal-topology-of-grid-cells.md) persistent-homology pipeline. If the toroidal manifold is intact and unchanged in sleep while the awake map is deformed, then the deformation is in the *input mapping* (from space to the torus), not in the *attractor*. That result would establish the synthesis I argue for in §2.5: a rigid toroidal path integrator, with a learned, predictive, environment-dependent map from the world onto it. I would bet on that outcome.
</details>

## 6. Open problems and what would settle them

**1. Are modules independent attractors, or one system?** The residue-code story requires modules to be *independent* — that is where the multiplicative range comes from. But modules are anatomically interleaved and could be coupled. If they are coupled, the effective code has far less than $\prod\lambda_m$ range. **What would settle it:** simultaneous recording of two modules with topological analysis of the *joint* state space. Independent modules give $\mathbb T^2\times\mathbb T^2=\mathbb T^4$ ($\beta = 1,4,6,4,1$). Coupled ones give something lower-dimensional. This is a direct extension of Gardner et al. and is technically feasible now.

**2. Who reads the grid code?** Every capacity argument assumes a decoder that exploits modularity, and — as Section 3 shows — even *error correction* requires an outlier-rejecting decoder, not just a linear readout. Decoding a redundant residue system is nontrivial in hardware, let alone in cortex. **What would settle it:** show that a downstream population actually resolves grid ambiguity in a way a linear readout of the grid population could not. Nobody has, and this is the largest unexamined assumption in Reading A′.

**3. How do goal and geometry effects get in?** Boccara et al. and Butler et al. show reward restructures grid maps; Krupic et al. show geometry shears them. In a rigid attractor these must enter through the *mapping* from world to torus — boundary-cell and landmark inputs and their plasticity — not through the attractor itself. **What would settle it:** the transparent-versus-passable barrier experiment of §2.5, combined with sleep topology. If the torus is invariant while the map deforms, the debate is over.

**4. Is the scale ratio explained or fitted?** Free choices in every normative account (decoder, noise model, dimensionality) move the predicted ratio anywhere from 1.4 to 2.7, and the observed value sits inside that range. That is not a test. **What would settle it:** a species comparison — animals with different behavioural ranges or module counts should have *predictably* different ratios. Bats are the obvious case, and 3-D grid structure is contested: Ginosar et al. (2021) report non-lattice 3-D fields in flying bats, a serious problem for any account that derives hexagons from an isotropic 2-D instability and expects an FCC/HCP lattice in 3-D.

**5. What is the level-1 problem?** A says "estimate position"; B says "estimate discounted future occupancy." Both are guesses. An underrated third: the grid system computes *displacement vectors* for vector-based navigation — the quantity you need to head home in a straight line, which is what path integration is behaviourally *for*. That specification predicts the natural readout is a *difference* of grid phases (goal minus current), not an absolute position. **What would settle it:** find the population representing grid-phase differences. Bush, Barry, Manson & Burgess (2015) argued on theoretical grounds that such a code must exist; object-vector and vector-trace cells are candidates, but the link has not been made.
