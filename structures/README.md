# Thread B — Mathematical structures born from circuits

> Neuroscience is usually cast as a consumer of mathematics. It is also, occasionally, a
> producer. This thread is a catalogue of the objects it has produced, and a method for
> recognizing the next one.

There is a difference between *applying* mathematics to a circuit and *being forced to invent*
mathematics by a circuit. Applying Fourier analysis to a receptive field is the first. Being
unable to characterize which binary codes arise from convex receptive fields, and therefore
constructing a new ideal in a polynomial ring whose canonical form encodes exactly the
combinatorial obstructions — that is the second.

The second is rarer, more interesting, and more learnable than it looks. It happens when a
circuit presents a question that the mathematics on the shelf cannot answer *in the form the
question is asked*. The catalogue below is organized so that you can see the shape of that
situation repeating.

---

## The eight generative situations

Each of these is a recognizable predicament. When you find yourself in one, you are probably
looking at a new object rather than a hard problem.

**1. The invariant problem.** *You can measure a quantity only up to an unknown nuisance
transformation, so no absolute quantity is meaningful and you must construct an invariant of the
group of transformations.* Neural correlations are related to underlying distances by an unknown
monotone function, so no correlation value means anything — but the *order* of the values does.
Hence [clique topology](S-02-clique-topology.md), an invariant of the ordering alone.

**2. The realizability problem.** *You observe a combinatorial object and want to know which
such objects can arise from an underlying geometric or physical constraint.* Which binary codes
$\mathcal{C} \subseteq \{0,1\}^n$ arise from arrangements of convex sets? This is a question
about the interaction of combinatorics and convexity that nobody had asked in that form. Hence
[convex codes and the neural ideal](S-01-convex-codes-and-the-neural-ideal.md).

**3. The capacity problem.** *Statistical mechanics knows how many points a classifier can
store; your objects are not points.* Real neural representations of an object under nuisance
variation are *manifolds*, not vectors. Extending Gardner's replica calculation from points to
manifolds requires new order parameters. Hence
[manifold capacity](S-04-manifold-capacity.md).

**4. The structure→dynamics dictionary.** *You want to read attractors off connectivity without
simulating.* Given a graph, what are the network's stable states? For threshold-linear dynamics
with combinatorially-specified weights this has actual theorems. Hence
[combinatorial threshold-linear networks](S-03-combinatorial-threshold-linear-networks.md) and,
in the continuous-statistics regime, [low-rank connectivity theory](S-08-low-rank-connectivity.md).

**5. The wrong-geometry problem.** *Your data has a natural metric that is not Euclidean, and
forcing it into $\mathbb{R}^d$ costs you exponentially.* Odour space is generated by tree-like
biochemical processes; trees embed in Euclidean space with unavoidable distortion but in
hyperbolic space with almost none. Hence [hyperbolic odour space](S-06-hyperbolic-odor-space.md).

**6. The empirical topology problem.** *Your theory predicts the shape of a state space and you
must verify it from noisy, unlabelled, high-dimensional data.* Continuous attractor theory says
grid-cell activity lives on a torus. Proving that from spike trains — with no access to the
"true" coordinates — requires persistent homology deployed as an inferential instrument. Hence
[the toroidal topology of grid cells](S-05-toroidal-topology-of-grid-cells.md).

**7. The missing formalism.** *There is no computational model at the level of abstraction where
the phenomenon lives.* Neither single-neuron models nor Turing machines describe what a
population of assemblies does. Hence [assembly calculus](S-09-assembly-calculus.md) — a formal
system with its own operations and its own completeness results.

**8. The generic-null problem.** *Before claiming your circuit is special, you must know what a
random circuit of the same statistics does — and nobody has computed it.* Answering it for
recurrent neural dynamics produced a body of theory (dynamic mean-field theory, structured
spectra) that outlived its original motivation. Hence
[random matrices and chaos](S-07-random-matrices-and-chaos.md), and its
[geometric shadow](S-10-tropical-and-piecewise-linear.md) in threshold-linear networks.

A ninth, which is really a corollary of 2 and 8: **the optimal-parameter problem** — when theory
predicts a *number* that biology can be measured against, as with the optimal in-degree of the
mushroom body's random projection. See
[expanders and optimal degree](S-11-expanders-and-optimal-degree.md).

## How to recognize one in the wild

A practical checklist. You are plausibly sitting on a new mathematical object when:

1. **You keep saying "up to."** "The correlations are related to distance up to an unknown
   monotone function." "The code is defined up to relabelling." Every "up to" is a group action,
   and every group action wants an invariant. If the invariant does not exist yet, build it.

2. **Your simulation works and you cannot say why.** A dictionary is missing. The gap between
   "I ran it and got sequences" and "this graph must produce sequences" is exactly where
   [S-03](S-03-combinatorial-threshold-linear-networks.md) came from.

3. **The natural null model is not available in closed form.** See
   [S-07](S-07-random-matrices-and-chaos.md). Computing your own null usually means developing
   theory, and the theory outlives the null.

4. **The objects you are storing/classifying/comparing are not the objects the existing theory
   handles.** Points vs. manifolds ([S-04](S-04-manifold-capacity.md)). Vectors vs. codes
   ([S-01](S-01-convex-codes-and-the-neural-ideal.md)). This is the highest-yield signal on the
   list.

5. **You are fighting the geometry.** If a representation needs exponentially many dimensions,
   or your embeddings always have stubborn residual distortion, the ambient space is wrong, not
   the method ([S-06](S-06-hyperbolic-odor-space.md)).

6. **The right level of description has no formal language.** If you cannot state your
   hypothesis without hand-waving about "assemblies" or "phases" or "channels," the formalism
   is missing, and writing it down *is* the contribution ([S-09](S-09-assembly-calculus.md)).

And one anti-pattern, worth as much as the six patterns: **importing a heavy formalism because
it is beautiful.** Category theory, sheaves, and information geometry have all been pointed at
neuroscience repeatedly, and the yield has been low relative to the effort, because the
formalism arrived before the question. Every entry in the catalogue above went the other way —
the question came first and the object was built to fit it, often in mathematically unglamorous
form. The neural ideal is not deep algebra. It is *the right* algebra, which is much harder.

## A note on how to study these

Each note is deliberately short and is meant to be read alongside its Thread-A case study. The
recommended order is the one in the [26-week plan](../README.md#a-suggested-26-week-pace), which
interleaves them so that you meet each object just after the circuit that produced it.

For each note, the exercise that matters is the last one, which asks you to apply the *situation*
rather than the *object*. The objects are worth knowing. The situations are worth internalizing.
