# S-01 — Convex codes and the neural ideal

> **The object.** The *neural ideal* $J_\mathcal{C}$ — an ideal in $\mathbb{F}_2[x_1,\dots,x_n]$
> whose canonical form is exactly the list of receptive-field containment relations forced by a
> combinatorial code — together with the classification problem for *convex codes*.
> **Born from.** Place cells with convex place fields; more generally, any population whose
> receptive fields are convex regions of a stimulus space.
> **Mathematical home.** Commutative algebra, discrete geometry, combinatorial topology.
> **Situation.** #2, the realizability problem.
> **Novelty.** **Genuinely new.** Nobody in convex geometry had asked which binary codes are
> realizable by arrangements of convex sets. The question has produced new theorems, new
> counterexamples, and open problems that remain open.

---

## 1. Where it comes from

A hippocampal place cell fires when the animal is inside a *place field* $U_i \subseteq X$,
where $X$ is the environment. Place fields are, empirically, roughly convex blobs. Now throw
away everything except the combinatorics: record which subsets of neurons are ever
simultaneously active. You get a **neural code**

$$\mathcal{C} \subseteq \{0,1\}^n,$$

a set of binary codewords. This is exactly what you can observe without knowing the animal's
position, and it is what a downstream area receives.

The question Curto and Itskov asked is the obvious one that nobody had asked: **which codes can
arise this way?** Formally, $\mathcal{C}$ has a *realization* by sets $U_1,\dots,U_n \subseteq
\mathbb{R}^d$ if for every $\sigma \subseteq [n]$,

$$\sigma \in \mathcal{C} \iff A^U_\sigma := \Big(\bigcap_{i \in \sigma} U_i\Big) \setminus
\Big(\bigcup_{j \notin \sigma} U_j\Big) \neq \emptyset.$$

$\mathcal{C}$ is a **convex code** if such a realization exists with every $U_i$ open and convex.

This is a purely mathematical question with a purely neuroscientific origin, and the answer is
genuinely hard. It matters because it is the cleanest possible statement of a question you
should always be asking: *what does the combinatorial structure of the code tell me about the
geometry of the stimulus space, without ever measuring the stimulus space?*

---

## 2. The object, precisely

### The neural ideal

Work in $\mathbb{F}_2[x_1,\dots,x_n]$, where $x_i$ is the indicator of neuron $i$ firing. Let

$$I_\mathcal{C} = \{f : f(c) = 0 \ \ \forall c \in \mathcal{C}\}$$

be the ideal of functions vanishing on the code, and $R_\mathcal{C} = \mathbb{F}_2[x]/I_\mathcal{C}$
the **neural ring**. Because we are over $\mathbb{F}_2$ with $x_i^2 = x_i$, $I_\mathcal{C}$ is
awkward to read. The useful object is the **neural ideal** $J_\mathcal{C}$, generated by the
*pseudo-monomials* it contains, where a pseudo-monomial is

$$x_\sigma \prod_{j \in \tau}(1 - x_j), \qquad x_\sigma := \prod_{i \in \sigma} x_i, \qquad
\sigma \cap \tau = \emptyset.$$

Here is the whole point, and it is a one-line calculation:

$$x_\sigma \prod_{j \in \tau}(1-x_j) \in J_\mathcal{C}
\iff \text{no codeword contains } \sigma \text{ and avoids } \tau
\iff \bigcap_{i \in \sigma} U_i \subseteq \bigcup_{j \in \tau} U_j .$$

So **each pseudo-monomial in the ideal is a receptive-field containment relation.** The
*canonical form* $\mathrm{CF}(J_\mathcal{C})$ — the set of minimal such pseudo-monomials — is a
complete, non-redundant list of the geometric relations the code forces. It is computable by an
explicit algorithm from $\mathcal{C}$.

That is the translation device: **combinatorics of the code $\to$ algebra of an ideal $\to$
geometry of the receptive fields.**

### Convexity and its obstructions

Convexity now becomes a constraint on which canonical forms are possible. The simplest
obstruction is a two-line argument that you should be able to reproduce cold:

Suppose $x_1(1-x_2)(1-x_3) \in J_\mathcal{C}$, i.e. $U_1 \subseteq U_2 \cup U_3$. Suppose also
$U_1 \cap U_2 \neq \emptyset$, $U_1 \cap U_3 \neq \emptyset$, and $U_1 \cap U_2 \cap U_3 =
\emptyset$. Then

$$U_1 = (U_1 \cap U_2) \sqcup (U_1 \cap U_3)$$

is a partition of $U_1$ into two nonempty disjoint open sets. So $U_1$ is disconnected, hence
not convex. **The code is non-convex, and we proved it without ever attempting a realization.**

The general version of this is the theory of **local obstructions**: for $\sigma$ a face of the
simplicial complex $\Delta(\mathcal{C})$ that is not itself a codeword, convexity forces the
link $\mathrm{Lk}_\sigma(\Delta(\mathcal{C}))$ to be contractible; a non-contractible link is an
obstruction. (This is a nerve-lemma argument: the nerve of a good cover by convex sets is
homotopy equivalent to the union.)

And then the field's most instructive result: **local obstructions are not sufficient.**
Lienkaemper, Shiu & Woodstock (2017) exhibit a code with no local obstructions that is
nevertheless not convex. There is also a separate, subtle open/closed distinction — the class of
open-convex codes and the class of closed-convex codes are different — and the **minimal
embedding dimension** problem (what is the smallest $d$?) remains largely open, constrained from
one side by Helly's theorem.

---

## 3. Why the neuroscience forced it

Standard tools were unavailable for a specific and instructive reason: **the stimulus space is
not observable.** You cannot measure the place fields directly; you measure co-firing. Every
existing tool in convex geometry takes the sets as given and asks about their intersections.
Here the intersection pattern is given and the sets are unknown. Inverting the question inverted
the mathematics, and the inverse problem had no existing machinery — hence a new ideal.

Note the shape of this, because it recurs: *the observable is a combinatorial shadow of a
geometric object, and you want to constrain the object from the shadow.* You are in Situation #2
whenever that sentence describes your data.

---

## 4. How to recognize the pattern elsewhere

Ask: **is my data a set of co-activation patterns, and do I believe the underlying tuning
regions have a geometric property?** If yes, you can run this machinery.

For olfaction this is a live and under-exploited question. Suppose receptor or PN tuning
"fields" are regions of some odour space $X$. Then the observed co-activation code
$\mathcal{C}$ constrains the geometry of $X$ — and in particular, *if the code turns out to be
non-convex in every dimension, the natural conclusion is not that the tuning curves are weird
but that the odour space is not $\mathbb{R}^d$*. Which is exactly the hypothesis of
[S-06](S-06-hyperbolic-odor-space.md). The two notes are the same question asked from opposite
ends, and joining them is, as far as I know, unpublished.

## 5. Exercises

**Ex S1.1 ★** — Show that $\mathcal{C} = \{\emptyset, \{1\},\{2\},\{3\},\{1,2\},\{1,3\}\}$ is
convex, by exhibiting a realization in $\mathbb{R}^1$.

<details markdown="1"><summary>Solution</summary>

Take $U_2 = (-1,1)$, $U_1 = (0,3)$, $U_3 = (2,4)$. Reading off the regions:

| interval | active set |
|---|---|
| $(-1,0)$ | $\{2\}$ |
| $(0,1)$ | $\{1,2\}$ |
| $(1,2)$ | $\{1\}$ |
| $(2,3)$ | $\{1,3\}$ |
| $(3,4)$ | $\{3\}$ |
| outside | $\emptyset$ |

Every codeword of $\mathcal{C}$ appears and nothing else does. All three sets are open intervals,
hence convex. So $\mathcal{C}$ is convex with minimal embedding dimension $1$.
</details>

**Ex S1.2 ★★** — Let $\mathcal{C} = \{\emptyset, \{2\},\{3\},\{1,2\},\{1,3\}\}$. Compute enough
of the canonical form to prove $\mathcal{C}$ is not convex.

<details markdown="1"><summary>Solution</summary>

Note $\{1\} \notin \mathcal{C}$: no codeword contains $1$ while avoiding both $2$ and $3$.
Therefore

$$x_1(1-x_2)(1-x_3) \in J_\mathcal{C} \iff U_1 \subseteq U_2 \cup U_3.$$

Also $\{1,2,3\} \notin \mathcal{C}$, and indeed no codeword contains all three, so
$x_1x_2x_3 \in J_\mathcal{C}$, i.e. $U_1 \cap U_2 \cap U_3 = \emptyset$.

Meanwhile $\{1,2\} \in \mathcal{C}$ gives $U_1 \cap U_2 \neq \emptyset$ and $\{1,3\} \in
\mathcal{C}$ gives $U_1 \cap U_3 \neq \emptyset$.

Hence $U_1 = (U_1 \cap U_2) \sqcup (U_1 \cap U_3)$, a disjoint union of two nonempty open sets:
$U_1$ is disconnected. A convex set is connected. Contradiction — $\mathcal{C}$ is not convex,
in any dimension.

This is the minimal local obstruction, and it is worth noticing how little work it took: the
non-realizability is visible in the code's *missing* codewords, not its present ones. Missing
codewords are the informative ones. Experimentally, this means your statistical power problem is
about establishing absences, which is uncomfortable but at least clearly stated.
</details>

**Ex S1.3 ★★** — Prove that if $\mathcal{C}$ is realizable by convex sets in $\mathbb{R}^d$ and
$\sigma \subseteq [n]$ has $|\sigma| \geq d+2$ with every $(d+1)$-subset of $\sigma$ in
$\Delta(\mathcal{C})$, then $\sigma \in \Delta(\mathcal{C})$.

<details markdown="1"><summary>Solution</summary>

This is Helly's theorem in disguise. $\tau \in \Delta(\mathcal{C})$ means
$\bigcap_{i\in\tau} U_i \neq \emptyset$.

Helly: for a finite family of convex sets in $\mathbb{R}^d$, if every $d+1$ of them have a common
point, then all of them do.

Apply it to $\{U_i\}_{i \in \sigma}$. The hypothesis says every $(d+1)$-subset has nonempty
intersection. Helly gives $\bigcap_{i \in \sigma} U_i \neq \emptyset$, i.e. $\sigma \in
\Delta(\mathcal{C})$. $\square$

**Consequence, which is the useful part:** $\Delta(\mathcal{C})$ must have no "empty
$(d+1)$-dimensional-looking" faces — the Helly number bounds the dimension from below. If you
observe a code whose simplicial complex has all $(d+1)$-subsets of some $\sigma$ present but
$\sigma$ absent, then **no realization in $\mathbb{R}^d$ exists** and the embedding dimension is
at least $d+1$. This gives you a genuine lower bound on the dimension of the stimulus space
computed from co-firing statistics alone, with no access to the stimulus. That is a remarkable
thing to be able to do, and it is the reason this note is in the course.
</details>

**Ex S1.4 ★★★** — *(Situation, not object.)* Formulate the analogous realizability question for
your own system. Identify: the combinatorial observable, the hypothesized geometric constraint,
and one code pattern whose presence would falsify the constraint. For olfaction, do it for PN
co-activation across an odour panel and the hypothesis that PN tuning regions are convex in a
Euclidean odour space.

<details markdown="1"><summary>Solution</summary>

Sketch of the intended answer for olfaction.

*Observable.* Binarize PN responses (above/below a response threshold) across a panel of $M$
odours. Each odour gives a codeword; $\mathcal{C}$ is the set of observed codewords. Note the
sampling problem immediately: $\mathcal{C}$ is a *sample* of the true code, so absent codewords
may be unsampled rather than impossible. This is the central methodological obstacle and must be
handled with an explicit sampling model — the theory as published assumes the code is known
exactly.

*Constraint.* PN $i$ responds to odours in a convex region $U_i \subseteq X$ for some odour
space $X \cong \mathbb{R}^d$.

*Falsifier.* Any local obstruction, e.g. the Ex S1.2 pattern: PNs $1,2,3$ with $\{1,2\}$ and
$\{1,3\}$ observed, $\{1\}$ and $\{1,2,3\}$ never observed across a dense panel. That forces PN
1's tuning region to be disconnected in *any* Euclidean odour space.

*Why this is interesting rather than pedantic.* The literature routinely assumes a Euclidean
odour space (it is implicit in every PCA of odour descriptors and every "odour space" MDS plot).
The convex-code machinery gives a way to test the assumption using only combinatorial response
data, with no commitment to a particular set of molecular descriptors — which is precisely the
step that is usually arbitrary and usually unexamined. A finding of robust non-convexity would
be direct evidence for the hyperbolic hypothesis of [S-06](S-06-hyperbolic-odor-space.md), since
convexity is a metric-dependent notion and geodesic convexity in $\mathbb{H}^d$ is a different
condition.
</details>

## 6. Reading

- **Curto, Itskov, Veliz-Cuba & Youngs (2013)**, *The neural ring: an algebraic tool for
  analyzing the intrinsic structure of neural codes* (Bull. Math. Biol.) — read it for: the
  definition of the neural ring and ideal, the canonical form, and the algorithm to compute it.
  This is the founding paper; read it first and slowly.
- **Curto, Itskov, Morrison, Roth & Walker (2013)**, on combinatorial codes and receptive field
  structure — read it for: the simplicial-complex perspective and the first obstruction results.
- **Giusti & Itskov (2014)**, on local obstructions to convexity — read it for: the nerve-lemma
  argument in its general form.
- **Curto, Gross, Jeffries, Morrison, Omar, Rosen, Shiu & Youngs (2017)**, *What makes a neural
  code convex?* (SIAM J. Appl. Algebra Geom.) — read it for: the state-of-the-art
  characterization and the sharpest statement of what local obstructions do and do not capture.
- **Lienkaemper, Shiu & Woodstock (2017)**, on obstructions to convexity beyond local ones —
  read it for: the counterexample. This is the paper that tells you the problem is genuinely
  hard rather than merely unfinished.
- **Curto (2017)**, *What can topology tell us about the neural code?* (Bull. AMS) — read it
  for: the survey, and the best single statement of why a neuroscientist should care about any
  of this. Start here if the algebra feels unmotivated.
