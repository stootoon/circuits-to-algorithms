# S-06 — Hyperbolic representational geometry
{: .no_toc }

> **The object.** Negatively-curved representational spaces — $\mathbb{H}^d$ and Gromov
> $\delta$-hyperbolicity — as the natural home for representations of hierarchically-generated
> stimuli, with olfaction the leading case.
> **Born from.** The failure of Euclidean embeddings to capture the structure of natural odour
> statistics and odour perception without a stubborn residual distortion.
> **Mathematical home.** Coarse geometry, hyperbolic geometry, metric embedding theory.

## 1. Where it comes from

Every "odour space" plot you have ever seen is Euclidean. MDS on perceptual descriptors, PCA on
molecular features, $t$-SNE on receptor activation patterns — all of them embed odours in
$\mathbb{R}^d$, because that is the default and nobody stated it as an assumption.

The assumption is load-bearing, and there is a specific reason to doubt it. Odorants in the
world are not drawn from a Gaussian cloud. They are **metabolic products**: molecules generated
by biochemical reaction networks in plants, animals, and microbes. Those networks are
approximately *hierarchical* — pathways branch, products of a common precursor share structure —
so the natural statistics of odour have a tree-like organization.

And trees do not fit in Euclidean space.

---

## 2. The object, precisely

### The embedding obstruction

The obstruction is a volume-growth argument, and it is worth having at your fingertips because
it applies far beyond olfaction.

A regular tree with branching factor $b$ has, at graph distance $r$ from the root, roughly $b^r$
nodes. To embed with bounded distortion, the target space must have room for $b^r$
approximately-equidistant points within radius $r$. But in $\mathbb{R}^d$ the volume of a ball
grows *polynomially*:

$$\mathrm{vol}\big(B_r \subset \mathbb{R}^d\big) \propto r^d.$$

Exponential demand, polynomial supply. So embedding a tree of depth $r$ into $\mathbb{R}^d$ with
distortion $O(1)$ requires $d$ growing with $r$ — the dimension must grow with the depth of the
hierarchy. This is a theorem, not a numerical inconvenience: Bourgain's embedding results give
the general bounds, and the distortion for trees in fixed-dimensional Euclidean space is
unavoidable.

Hyperbolic space $\mathbb{H}^d$ has exponential volume growth:

$$\mathrm{vol}\big(B_r \subset \mathbb{H}^d\big) \sim e^{(d-1)r}.$$

Supply matches demand. And indeed **any tree embeds into $\mathbb{H}^2$ with arbitrarily low
distortion** (Sarkar's construction, following Gromov). Two dimensions suffice, for a tree of
any depth. That is a spectacular difference from the Euclidean case and it is the whole reason
this note exists.

### $\delta$-hyperbolicity: the diagnostic

You do not need to guess. Gromov's $\delta$-hyperbolicity is a computable property of a finite
metric space, via the **four-point condition**. For points $w,x,y,z$, form the three pairwise-sum
quantities

$$d(w,x)+d(y,z), \quad d(w,y)+d(x,z), \quad d(w,z)+d(x,y),$$

and let $M_1 \geq M_2 \geq M_3$ be them sorted. The space is $\delta$-hyperbolic if

$$M_1 - M_2 \leq 2\delta \quad \text{for all quadruples.}$$

Trees are $0$-hyperbolic. $\mathbb{H}^d$ is $\delta$-hyperbolic with $\delta$ a small constant.
Euclidean space of dimension $\geq 2$ is *not* $\delta$-hyperbolic for any finite $\delta$ (as
you take larger configurations $\delta$ grows without bound). So the normalized statistic

$$\hat{\delta} = \frac{\langle M_1 - M_2\rangle}{2\,\langle d \rangle}$$

over sampled quadruples is a scale-free number: near $0$ means tree-like, larger means
Euclidean-like. **This is a one-line diagnostic you can run on any dissimilarity matrix you
have**, and I would encourage running it on every representational similarity matrix you
possess before you next fit an MDS.

### The olfactory result

Zhou, Smith & Sharpee (2018) analyzed the statistics of natural odour mixtures (concentrations
of volatile compounds measured in natural samples) and of human olfactory perceptual
descriptors, and found in both cases that the correlation/similarity structure is better
accounted for by hyperbolic than Euclidean geometry — with a low-dimensional hyperbolic
embedding capturing what required many more Euclidean dimensions. The proposed mechanistic
origin is exactly the one above: odorants are generated by branching metabolic networks, so
odour space inherits their tree-like structure.

Sharpee has since argued the point more generally: hyperbolic geometry may be the right default
for neural representations of hierarchically-structured stimuli, not a curiosity of olfaction.

## 3. Why the neuroscience forced it

Hyperbolic geometry was not invented for this and needed no development. What the neuroscience
forced was the *diagnosis* — the recognition that a stubborn residual in Euclidean embeddings is
not noise, not "we need more dimensions," but a signature of curvature.

That is a distinct and underrated kind of contribution: not new mathematics, but the correct
identification of which existing mathematics the phenomenon belongs to. It required knowing that
exponential volume growth is a thing, that trees are the canonical example, and that odorants
come from metabolic networks. The insight lives at the junction of three literatures and could
not have come from any one.

---

## 4. How to recognize the pattern elsewhere

**Symptoms of the wrong geometry:**

- Your embedding needs many more dimensions than the phenomenon seems to warrant, and adding
  dimensions keeps helping without an obvious stopping point.
- Residual stress in MDS plateaus at a stubbornly high value.
- Your data has a known generative process that is branching or hierarchical.
- Distances behave strangely: many points are "far from everything," or the distance
  distribution is bimodal in a way flat geometry does not produce.

**The test:** compute $\hat{\delta}$. It costs ten lines.

**The candidates beyond hyperbolic:** spherical/positively-curved (for cyclic or bounded
variables — orientation, hue, head direction, and note that
[C4](../part2-case-studies/C4-continuous-attractors.md)'s ring is exactly this); product spaces
(hyperbolic $\times$ spherical, natural when a stimulus has both hierarchical and cyclic
structure); and general Riemannian manifolds fitted from data.

**Where this connects in the course.** Note the tension with
[S-01](S-01-convex-codes-and-the-neural-ideal.md): convexity is metric-dependent, and a set that
is geodesically convex in $\mathbb{H}^d$ need not be convex in any Euclidean embedding of the
same points. So a neural code that appears *non-convex* under the Euclidean assumption may be
perfectly convex hyperbolically. Testing whether locust or *Drosophila* PN codes are
hyperbolic-convex but not Euclidean-convex would tie the two structure notes together, and I
believe it is unpublished.

## 5. Exercises

**Ex S6.1 ★** — Implement $\hat{\delta}$ and calibrate it on a tree, on Euclidean points, and on
points sampled from the Poincaré disk.

<details markdown="1"><summary>Solution</summary>

```python
import numpy as np
from itertools import combinations

def delta_hat(D, n_samples=20000, rng=np.random.default_rng(0)):
    n = D.shape[0]; vals = []
    for _ in range(n_samples):
        w,x,y,z = rng.choice(n, 4, replace=False)
        M = sorted([D[w,x]+D[y,z], D[w,y]+D[x,z], D[w,z]+D[x,y]])
        vals.append(M[2]-M[1])
    return np.mean(vals) / (2*D[np.triu_indices_from(D,1)].mean())

# (a) balanced binary tree, graph distances -> expect ~0
# (b) 200 iid Gaussian points in R^10, Euclidean distance -> expect notably larger
# (c) points in the Poincare disk, hyperbolic distance:
def poincare_dist(u, v):
    num = 2*np.sum((u-v)**2, -1)
    den = (1-np.sum(u**2,-1))*(1-np.sum(v**2,-1))
    return np.arccosh(1 + num/den)
```

You should find $\hat\delta \approx 0$ for the tree, small for the Poincaré-disk sample, and
substantially larger for the Euclidean sample. Calibrate on *your* $n$ and dimension before
interpreting a real dataset — $\hat\delta$ has finite-sample bias and depends on the sampling
distribution, so the number is only meaningful against matched synthetic controls. This is the
single most common error in applied $\delta$-hyperbolicity: reporting an absolute value without
a matched null.
</details>

**Ex S6.2 ★★** — Prove the volume-growth obstruction quantitatively: show that embedding the
complete binary tree of depth $r$ into $\mathbb{R}^d$ requires distortion growing with $r$ for
fixed $d$.

<details markdown="1"><summary>Solution</summary>

Counting argument. In the tree $T_r$, the $2^r$ leaves are pairwise at distance between $2$ and
$2r$; in particular there are $N = 2^r$ points with pairwise distances all $\geq 2$ and all
$\leq 2r$.

Suppose an embedding $f$ into $\mathbb{R}^d$ has distortion $\Delta$, normalized so that
$d_T(u,v) \leq \|f(u)-f(v)\| \leq \Delta\, d_T(u,v)$. Then the images of the leaves are $N$
points with pairwise distances $\geq 2$ contained in a ball of radius $\approx \Delta \cdot 2r$.

A packing bound: the number of points with pairwise distance $\geq 2$ in a ball of radius $R$ in
$\mathbb{R}^d$ is at most $(1 + R)^d$ up to constants (volume of the big ball divided by volume
of radius-1 balls around each point). So

$$2^r \;\lesssim\; (1 + 2\Delta r)^d
\;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; r\log 2 \lesssim d \log(2\Delta r)
\;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; \Delta \gtrsim \frac{2^{r/d}}{r}.$$

For fixed $d$, distortion grows **exponentially** in $r/d$. Equivalently, keeping distortion
$O(1)$ forces $d = \Omega(r/\log r)$: the dimension must grow with the depth of the hierarchy.
$\square$

Contrast $\mathbb{H}^2$: Sarkar's construction embeds any tree with distortion $1+\epsilon$ in
two dimensions, by placing children of each node on a circle around it, using the exponential
growth of hyperbolic circumference to keep them far apart. The exponential supply exactly meets
the exponential demand.

Now the punchline for coding: **if odour space is genuinely tree-like with depth $r$, a
Euclidean neural code needs $\Omega(r/\log r)$ dimensions to represent it faithfully, while a
hyperbolic code needs a handful.** That is not an aesthetic preference; it is a resource
argument about how many neurons you need, which is the kind of statement a level-1 theory should
make.
</details>

**Ex S6.3 ★★★** — *(Situation, not object.)* Take a representational similarity matrix from your
own work. Compute $\hat\delta$ against matched Euclidean and hyperbolic nulls. If it looks
hyperbolic, propose the generative hierarchy responsible. If it looks Euclidean, say what that
rules out.

<details markdown="1"><summary>Solution</summary>

Open. Notes on doing it honestly:

*Matched nulls are mandatory.* Generate synthetic data with the same $n$, same dimension, same
sampling density, and same noise level, from (a) Euclidean and (b) hyperbolic ground truth, and
push both through your *exact* pipeline including any correlation-to-distance transformation.
Report $\hat\delta$ for your data between these two calibration points. An absolute
$\hat\delta$ is uninterpretable.

*The nuisance-transformation problem bites here too.* Converting correlations to distances
requires a monotone map you do not know — the same problem that motivated
[S-02](S-02-clique-topology.md). Since $\hat\delta$ is *not* invariant under monotone
transformations of the distances (it uses sums of distances, so it depends on the scale), you
must either justify the transformation or report sensitivity across a family of them. This is a
real weakness of the method and you should state it rather than bury it.

*If hyperbolic:* name the hierarchy. For odour, metabolic pathway structure. For faces,
taxonomic/identity hierarchy. For language, the semantic hierarchy (which is where Nickel &
Kiela's Poincaré embeddings started). A hyperbolic finding without a proposed generative
hierarchy is a curve fit, not an explanation.

*If Euclidean:* that rules out a strongly hierarchical generative process dominating the
similarity structure — which for olfaction would be a genuinely interesting negative result,
since it would push against the metabolic-origin story and toward a receptor-driven,
combinatorial account of odour similarity.
</details>

## 6. Reading

- **Zhou, Smith & Sharpee (2018)**, *Hyperbolic geometry of the olfactory space* (Science
  Advances) — read it for: the primary result on natural odour statistics and perceptual
  descriptors. This is the paper for your purposes.
- **Sharpee (2019)**, *An argument for hyperbolic geometry in neural circuits* (Curr. Opin.
  Neurobiol.) — read it for: the general case, beyond olfaction, and the coding-efficiency
  argument.
- **Gromov (1987)**, *Hyperbolic groups* — read it for: the four-point condition and
  $\delta$-hyperbolicity at the source. Heavy; the definitions are what you need, not the proofs.
- **Sarkar (2011)**, on low-distortion Delaunay embedding of trees in hyperbolic space — read
  it for: the constructive theorem that trees fit in $\mathbb{H}^2$. Short and satisfying.
- **Nickel & Kiela (2017)**, *Poincaré embeddings for learning hierarchical representations*
  (NeurIPS) — read it for: how the machine-learning community independently arrived at the same
  place, and for practical optimization on hyperbolic manifolds if you want to fit one.
- **Bourgain (1985)**, on Lipschitz embedding of finite metric spaces into Hilbert space — read
  it for: the general distortion bounds that make the obstruction rigorous.
